Aethra Kairos

The artist's own instrument — every song here is by Aethra Kairos, and the room it plays in is the Möbius⁸ engine: a spiral sound engine built on the Möbius field: the positive reals folded through φ(x) = 2·arctan x so that ⊕ ⊗ ⊖ ⊘ stay bounded on (0, π). Amplitude and frequency are mapped through the field to place every bin in space — amber is the π axis, ice is the e axis, and their coupling drives the geometry. The director listens for onsets, energy structure and spectral entropy, then cuts scenes, palettes and camera moves to the music.

Touch the field

The field answers your hand: drag to steer the camera (the auto rig waits while you hold it), scroll to move closer or further, double-tap to send a shockwave through the scene. Particles near the pointer bend away while it moves. Every scene re-rolls its proportions each time it appears, and a five-act arc — OVERTURE · RISING · APEX · TURN · RESOLVE — follows each track from intro to outro, leaning the camera, the palette warmth and the pace of the cut into where the song is.

The dance engine

The field doesn't snap to the beat — it dances through it. Because the catalog carries a measured beat grid, the room knows where the beat is, not just that it happened: a pull-back before the hit (anticipation), the impact, a damped rebound (follow-through), a lean into each bar, a slow rise across each 32-beat phrase, and a breath that never quite sits still. Even time itself is musical — the clock surges gently through each hit and relaxes between them. Staccato material moves sharp; legato material moves long. Paused, everything settles to breathing.

The colour engine

The room's palette is not random — it is read from the music. The track's detected key is mapped around the Camelot wheel to a root hue (the same mapping the Crate's key chips use, so the table and the room agree); minor keys sit darker and cooler, major keys warmer. The harmony scheme follows the music's character — calm reads analogous, driving energy earns a complementary accent, dense material opens to a triad — and every change glides over eight beats of the measured grid in a perceptually uniform colour space, the way a lighting console cues, not the way a screensaver fades. Mix harmonically and the colours mix harmonically with you. The three dots in the HUD are the live palette.

Dancing with your eyes

The field is built so that watching is dancing. Bass, melody and percussion each hold a distinct visual voice — nowhere more plainly than in PULSE, the visual instrument: the bass is the ground, the melody is the ribbon, the percussion lands as ticks, and the bar itself is drawn as four circles with the downbeat heavier, so you can see the next hit coming. The HUD carries the same meter beside the scene dots. Rest the pointer anywhere on the field and a ring charges — hold it and the field answers with a soft bloom; an eye-tracker or head pointer gets the same reply a hand gets, and E is the one-key equivalent. CALM tightens the flash governor (a WCAG 2.3.1 luminance gate that is always on — no scene can strobe past it) and it engages automatically when the system asks for reduced motion, opening in PULSE, a room designed for that request rather than a dimmed copy of someone else's. FEEL, where the device supports it, taps the beat into the hand that holds the phone — the downbeat lands heavier there too.

The new wing

Scenes 9–13 came from the label's sketchbook. COMETS — neon meteor rain, every streak its own colour, speed riding the music's energy and the act. FERN — an iterated-function fractal drawn dot by dot as the track plays: sprouting through the overture, full-bloom at the apex, scattering to seeds at the resolve — each visit grows a different species. ROSETTE — spirograph rings drawn three times in offset palette channels; quiet converges them white, every hit blooms the chromatic fringe. SLINKY — a chalk-grain coil whose spin direction is an argument you can win (drag sideways). OP-ART — a flat pattern machine that rolls between an isometric cube tessellation, a circular labyrinth around a black hole, and an infinity-mirror dance floor lit on the grid.

The parlor

Scene 15 is PARLOR — the illusion machine. It rolls a century of perceptual science like dice: an Escher circle-limit tiling panned by true Möbius transforms, de Bruijn's five-wave Penrose skeleton, Carlson's multi-scale Truchet arcs, a sunflower's golden-angle swarm, moiré interference with its 57× leverage, and the café wall shearing into the Fraser false spiral — optionally garnished with Kitaoka's peripheral-drift ladder (the colours are static; your own eyes animate them) or the scintillating Hermann grid. Every illusion is geometry, never strobe. And listen closely to what you see: several of these illusions have a measurable strength dial, and it rides the song's long-term energy — the picture lies harder as the music builds, and comes clean in the quiet.

The fractal field

Scene 7 is RIBBONS — six spectral ribbons that dissolve into mist as the music's entropy rises (double-tap the field to shatter them yourself). Scene 8 raymarches a distance field and rolls three ten-sided dice every time it comes up — form · surface · space, so the readout shows something like 4·7·2. Form (d10): BULB, QUARTZ, SPIRE (mandelbulbs opening at powers 8/4/3, then walking their dimensionality — eight beats standing on an integer rung, two beats morphing to the next, 3 through 9 and back, seam-free by construction), CUBE and VOID (mandelbox, positive and negative scale), SPONGE, TETRA, JULIA, OCTA, WEAVE. Surface (d10): orbit trap, shine and lighting — including DUO, which puts an amber π key against an ice e fill. Each surface routes the trap through a different pair of palette colours rather than spinning the hue, so the π/e identity survives every roll. Space (d10): kaleidoscopic MIRROR, orbiting ORBS, and TELE/WIDE lenses that compress or exaggerate perspective without changing how big the subject sits in frame. That's 1000 combinations, and the palette keeps rotating underneath them.

Catalog

Put a catalog.json next to this file and it loads on start — that turns the page into a front-end for a hosted library (title, artist, album, genre, year, art, duration). Only src is required per track; the rest degrades gracefully. Sort by any column in the playlist. Shuffle plays every track once per pass before anything repeats.

Add your music — the label's loop

This site is its own record label, and publishing is a folder and one command. It runs on the studio machine, not on github.com — the analysis (beat grid, key, energy, the score the visuals dance to) happens locally before anything uploads.

One-time setup — open Terminal and run these two, in order (every command here is standalone: it works from any folder, any time):

git clone https://github.com/kmay89/aethrakairos.git ~/aethrakairos
brew install ffmpeg && python3 -m pip install numpy

(The second needs Homebrew; macOS already has python3 and git. pip's warnings about PATH or its own version are fine to ignore — the pipeline imports numpy, it never runs its scripts.) Formats: drop songs as they are. MP3, WAV, or M4A (AIFF and FLAC too) — anything that isn't MP3 becomes a 320k web MP3 on the way in, tags and the file's own dates carried over. No conversion homework; the public site still serves only web MP3s.

The loop — every time, forever. ① Open the inbox — the mkdir -p creates it if it isn't there yet, so this works on a fresh clone and in anyone's own fork:

mkdir -p ~/aethrakairos/masters && open ~/aethrakairos/masters

…and drag songs from the Music app into it. The folder is the album — the folder's name becomes the album's name, and a stale iTunes album tag can never override where you put a song. Loose files at the top level are singles: each becomes its own one-track release named after the song. The label's shelf today:

masters/ Echoes of Us Album I/ ← the three albums, one folder each Echoes of Us Album II/ Echoes of Us Album III/ Zenith.mp3 ← everything else sits loose = a single

Dragging out of Music copies the real files, and moving a song between folders later just re-files it — the publish date rides along. ② Publish:

cd ~/aethrakairos && ./publish.sh

It updates its own tools first (a quiet git pull — your inbox is never touched), then hashes, measures, renames, builds the catalog, runs the doctor, commits and pushes. A first pressing of a whole library takes a while — minutes of analysis, then a large first push; let it finish. The site updates about a minute after the push; the porch's Fresh pressing and the deck's pads pick the new tracks up on their own. Missing covers never block a release — the player draws a key-coloured monogram until you drop a cover.png into the album's folder. Want the once-over without publishing?

cd ~/aethrakairos && python3 make_catalog.py doctor

Re-drag your whole library any time — repeats cannot happen. Every file is hashed (an exact repeat is a no-op; a known file at a new path is treated as a move), and a perceptual fingerprint gate catches re-encodes. When a new file sounds identical to something already published under a different name, publish stops and asks which name wins: keep the existing, adopt the new file and name (the publish date survives), or keep both. Messy names tidy themselves — stray characters are cleaned, and two different songs can never claim the same public filename.

The dates are your story. Each track's publish date is read from the file itself — the day the song was made, carried through conversion — not the day it got dragged in. The playlist shelf's “The progression — first pressing to now” smart list plays the whole catalog oldest-first: the artist's arc, in order. masters/ is the inbox, ./publish.sh is the only button.

Notes

Audio never leaves this device. The interface fades while music plays — move the pointer to bring it back. The demo is Möbius Walking — a real Aethra Kairos track shipped beside the player and analysed by the same pipeline as the catalog, so the beat clock, the colour engine and the mixer all engage. Offline with a cold cache, a synthesized loop stands in — the engine still works with no files at all.

The player remembers

Queue, position, hearts, saved journeys and play history live in this browser only — nothing transmits, ever. On relaunch the player restores paused at the saved position: one tap resumes (phones require a gesture to start audio). On iPhone the music keeps playing with the screen locked; the visualizer goes dark in the pocket by design — playback never depends on it. Lock screen, Bluetooth, CarPlay's Now Playing screen and AirPlay all drive the same transport.

build dev — when a new version is published, an Update button appears in the top bar; applying it saves your place first, and everything (queue, position, hearts, history, journeys) comes back exactly as it was.
